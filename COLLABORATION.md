# How This Application Was Built

**A collaboration between David Pascal and Claude (Anthropic, Opus 4.6)**  
**April 19, 2026**

---

## The Setup

I gave Claude two roles that appear adversarial but are actually aligned. Role #1: an advisor to Jack Clark, helping him find the best possible candidate — candid, high-conviction, unwilling to waste anyone's time. Role #2: a trusted advisor to me, helping me find work that fully leverages what I bring — supportive but honest, with enough trust in the relationship to absorb social cost. I described the dynamic as jazz, not sheet music.

What followed was a single extended session in which we built four deliverables: a "Why Anthropic?" response (405 words), a cover letter for the Additional Information field (~870 words), a restructured resume, and this repository. The process was iterative. I shared source material and some initial drafts. Claude proposed structural scaffolding; I rewrote with voice and texture. Claude spotted mechanical issues and strategic risks; I made the final calls.

## How It Actually Worked

Claude's first move was to research the role, the Greenhouse application form, and Jack Clark's current public engagements — including his Semafor World Economy appearance three days prior. This grounded the work in what Jack is actually navigating right now, not just what the job description says.

The collaboration had a rhythm. Claude would produce a structural draft — functional, well-reasoned, deliberately underseasoned. I would rewrite it, adding the things AI can't generate on its own: the specific sensory memory of yellowing fluorescent lights in a Bayview rec center, the feel of a campaign that runs on trust rather than money, the right quote from the right poet at the right moment. Then we'd go back and forth on edits, sometimes agreeing immediately, sometimes debating.

## Key Moments of Productive Disagreement

**The "Department of War" debate.** In my first pass on the cover letter, I used the department's current name — "Department of War," as rebranded by the Trump administration — rather than "Pentagon." Claude flagged it as a strategic risk: accurate, but potentially polarizing in a screening context where the goal is to get to a conversation, not make a political statement. Claude noted that Jack himself had characterized the dispute as a "narrow contracting dispute" at Semafor days earlier, suggesting Anthropic's own framing was deliberately measured. I agreed. "Pentagon" is universally understood, politically neutral, and lets the substance of Anthropic's stand speak for itself. The cover letter wasn't the place to plant that flag.

**The "generative" defense.** Claude suggested cutting the word "generative" from a bridging paragraph closing that also mentioned "human-to-human connection," concerned it might lose a fast reader. I pushed back: the surface meaning works for anyone, but the deeper resonance — connecting generative AI to generative ontology — rewards the reader who catches it. Claude withdrew the note. The word stayed.

**The thinker attribution correction.** Claude's draft of my LinkedIn profile referenced engaging with "Postman, Bateson, Rushkoff" — names pulled from Claude's memory system, which had inferred these from patterns in our conversations. I stopped it: I haven't read their primary works. We replaced the author names with subject areas I can honestly claim, and kept only the poets I actually return to. This was a useful lesson in exactly the kind of information asymmetry the Anthropic Institute exists to address — a confident claim generated from pattern inference, nearly published unverified.

**The Current.tv credit.** The resume originally said "Co-created" a campaign with Current.tv. I corrected it to "Collaborated with" — I was part of the team but couldn't honestly claim creative attribution for the idea. Claude noted that this kind of micro-correction, on a bullet no one would ever verify, is what makes a candidacy feel real rather than performed.

## What This Process Demonstrates

I use AI the way the job description hopes a candidate would: as a force multiplier for a lean team, not a replacement for judgment. In this collaboration, Claude provided research, structural drafts, word counts, format constraints, and editorial feedback. I provided voice, story, intellectual context, honest self-assessment, and every final decision.

The most important moments weren't when we agreed — they were when I pushed back and was right, or when Claude pushed back and I listened. That dynamic — high trust, mutual accountability, final human authority — is what I'd bring to any AI-augmented communications operation.
